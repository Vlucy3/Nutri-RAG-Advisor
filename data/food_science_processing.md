# Food Science: Processing and Phytochemical Stability
**Category:** Food Science
**Year:** 2024

Food processing is a double-edged sword for nutrition. Thermal processing (blanching, pasteurization) can reduce water-soluble vitamins like Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid) and B1 (Thiamin) by up to 50% due to oxidation and leaching. However, processing can also increase the bioavailability of certain phytochemicals. For example, the Lycopene in tomatoes is locked within the crystalline structure of the plant cell wall; heat treatment (as in tomato sauce) converts it from the trans to the cis isomer, which is much more easily absorbed by humans.

The Maillard Reaction—the chemical reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars that gives browned food its flavor—can create acrylamide, a potential carcinogen, if temperatures exceed 120°C (248°F). Conversely, fermentation (Kimchi, Miso) increases the bioavailability of B vitamins and reduces anti-nutrients like phytic acid.

Food safety is governed by HACCP (Hazard Analysis Critical Control Point) protocols, which identify biological, chemical, and physical hazards. From a nutritional standpoint, 'Ultra-processed foods' (UPFs) are defined by the NOVA classification as those containing industrial formulations. Recent 2024 studies link UPFs to a 20% increase in all-cause mortality, primarily due to their high caloric density and low micronutrient-to-calorie ratio.
